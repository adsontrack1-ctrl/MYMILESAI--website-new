How to Track Mileage for Taxes: The Complete 2026 Guide

Step-by-step guide covering IRS and CRA requirements, what counts as deductible mileage, and the best methods for tracking your business drives.

Guide March 10, 2026 10 min read

If you drive for business, tracking your mileage is one of the simplest ways to reduce your tax bill. The IRS allows a deduction of $0.725 per business mile in 2026, and the CRA allows $0.73 per kilometre for the first 5,000 km. That means a freelancer who drives 12,000 miles per year could save over $8,700 in deductions — but only if you have the records to prove it.

This guide covers everything you need to know about tracking mileage for taxes in both the United States and Canada.

Why Mileage Tracking Matters

The IRS and CRA both require documentation of business driving to claim deductions. Without a proper mileage log, your deduction can be disallowed entirely in an audit. Estimates or reconstructions from memory don't qualify — you need contemporaneous records, meaning you log trips at or near the time they happen.

What Counts as Deductible Business Mileage

Understanding what qualifies saves you from costly mistakes:

Deductible trips:

  • Driving from your office to a client meeting
  • Trips between two work locations
  • Driving to the bank, post office, or supply store for business
  • From a qualifying home office to any business destination
  • All active miles on gig platforms (Uber, DoorDash, Skip The Dishes)
  • Driving to temporary work locations (expected under one year)

Not deductible:

  • Your regular commute from home to a fixed workplace
  • Personal errands (grocery store, gym, school pickup)
  • Vacation travel, even with some business mixed in

Four Ways to Track Mileage

1. GPS Mileage Tracking App (Recommended)

A GPS app runs in the background on your phone, automatically detects when you start driving, and logs every trip with date, time, route, distance, and location data. You classify each trip as business or personal, and at tax time you export a compliant report.

Pros: Automatic, accurate, audit-proof, no manual effort. Cons: Requires a smartphone, small battery impact.

2. Spreadsheet or Mileage Log Template

You manually enter each trip into a spreadsheet with date, destination, purpose, and miles driven. Templates are available as free downloads.

Pros: Free, no app needed. Cons: Easy to forget trips, time-consuming, prone to errors.

3. Paper Logbook

The traditional approach — a notebook in your car where you write down each trip as it happens.

Pros: Simple, no technology. Cons: Very easy to lose, hard to total at year end, no GPS verification.

4. Odometer-Only Method

Record your odometer at the start and end of each year, then estimate the business-use percentage.

Pros: Minimal effort. Cons: Least accurate, weakest in an audit, IRS prefers detailed trip logs.

What the IRS Requires in Your Mileage Log

To claim the standard mileage rate deduction, your log must include four things for each trip:

  • Date of the trip
  • Destination (where you drove)
  • Business purpose (client meeting, delivery, showing, etc.)
  • Miles driven

You should also record your odometer reading at the start and end of the tax year, and calculate your total annual miles and business-use percentage.

What the CRA Requires (Canada)

The CRA has similar but slightly different requirements. Your logbook must show:

  • Date, destination, and purpose for each business trip
  • Kilometres driven for each trip
  • Odometer readings at the start and end of the year
  • Total kilometres (business + personal)

The CRA recommends keeping a full logbook for one complete year, then a three-month sample in subsequent years, as long as the business-use percentage stays within 10% of your base year.

Standard Mileage Rate vs. Actual Expenses

Standard rate ($0.725/mile US, $0.73/km Canada): Multiply your business miles/km by the rate. Covers gas, insurance, depreciation, maintenance in one number. Simpler, works for most people.

Actual expenses: Track every vehicle cost (gas, oil, insurance, repairs, lease payments, depreciation) and deduct the business-use percentage. More work, potentially higher deduction if you drive an expensive vehicle.

For most self-employed professionals, the standard rate is simpler and results in a comparable deduction. Run both calculations to see which is higher for your situation.

Common Mistakes That Cost You Money

  • Not starting until tax season. The IRS requires records made at the time of travel, not from memory in April.
  • Forgetting short trips. A 3-mile trip to the bank doesn't seem like much, but done daily it's $570/year in deductions.
  • Missing deadhead miles. For gig drivers, miles between deliveries and heading to pickup zones are deductible.
  • Logging commute miles. Home-to-office commute is never deductible unless you have a qualifying home office.
  • Using a single annual estimate. The IRS wants individual trip records, not one number at year end.

How to Start Tracking Today

The best approach is to install a GPS mileage tracking app and let it run automatically. Every trip is logged with time, route, and distance. At the end of each day or week, review and classify your trips. At tax time, export the report and send it to your accountant.

Look for an app with automatic trip detection, offline GPS capability, AI-based classification, and one-tap export in PDF or CSV format. These features ensure you never miss a deductible mile.
