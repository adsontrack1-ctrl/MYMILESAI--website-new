CRA Mileage Rate 2026: Complete Canadian Guide

Everything Canadian professionals need to know about the 2026 automobile allowance rate, vehicle expense deductions, and CRA logbook requirements.

Canada Tax March 5, 2026 7 min read

The CRA automobile allowance rate for 2026 is $0.73 (73 cents) per kilometre for the first 5,000 business kilometres, and $0.67 per kilometre after that. If you drive in the Northwest Territories, Yukon, or Nunavut, the rate is $0.77/km for the first 5,000 km. This is the amount you can deduct per kilometre driven for business purposes on your Canadian tax return.

The rate is set annually by the Canada Revenue Agency and reflects the average cost of operating a vehicle in Canada, including fuel, insurance, depreciation, maintenance, and financing costs.

The two-tier structure means your first 5,000 business kilometres are worth more per kilometre than the rest. For a Canadian professional who drives 15,000 km per year for business, that works out to: (5,000 × $0.72) + (10,000 × $0.66) = $3,600 + $6,600 = $10,200 in deductions.

Who Can Claim Vehicle Expenses in Canada?

  • Self-employed individuals — freelancers, sole proprietors, independent contractors, and gig workers claim on Form T2125
  • Commission-based employees — if your employer signs a T2200 form confirming you need your own vehicle for work
  • Real estate agents — kilometres driven to showings, open houses, and client meetings
  • Delivery and rideshare drivers — all kilometres driven while actively working (Uber, Skip The Dishes, DoorDash, Instacart)
  • Sales representatives — territory coverage, client visits, and trade shows
  • Trades and contractors — driving to job sites, supply stores, and inspections
Note: Salaried employees who are not required to use their own vehicle for work generally cannot claim vehicle expenses. You need a signed T2200 from your employer to be eligible.

Simplified Method vs. Detailed Method

The CRA offers two ways to calculate your vehicle expense deduction:

Simplified method (per-kilometre rate): Multiply your business kilometres by the CRA rate. This is the easiest approach and works well for most people. No need to track individual expenses like gas receipts.

Detailed method (actual expenses): Track all actual vehicle costs for the year (fuel, insurance, maintenance, lease payments, depreciation, parking, etc.) and multiply by your business-use percentage (the ratio of business kilometres to total kilometres). More work, but potentially a higher deduction if you have an expensive vehicle.

Your business-use percentage = (Business km ÷ Total km) × 100. If you drove 20,000 km total and 12,000 were for business, your business-use percentage is 60%.

CRA Logbook Requirements

The CRA requires you to maintain a vehicle logbook that records:

  • Date of each business trip
  • Destination and business purpose
  • Kilometres driven for each trip
  • Odometer reading at the start and end of each year
  • Total kilometres driven in the year (business + personal)

The CRA recommends keeping a full logbook for at least one complete 12-month period to establish your business-use percentage. In subsequent years, you can keep a shorter sample period (at least three consecutive months) and use it to project your annual business use, as long as the percentage is within 10% of your base year.

A mileage tracking app that automatically logs your trips with GPS is the most reliable way to maintain a CRA-compliant logbook. Manual tracking often leads to missed trips and incomplete records that may not withstand a CRA audit.

What Kilometres Are Deductible?

Deductible:

  • Driving from your business location to a client site
  • Driving between two work locations
  • Trips to the bank, supplier, or post office for business
  • Driving from a home office (principal place of business) to clients
  • All kilometres while actively working gig apps

Not deductible:

  • Your regular commute between home and a fixed workplace
  • Personal errands (groceries, gym, school)
  • Vacation travel

Tips to Maximize Your Canadian Mileage Deduction

  • Start tracking from day one. Every business kilometre counts. A 5 km trip to a client adds up to $1,872 per year if done daily.
  • Track deadhead kilometres. For gig drivers, kilometres between deliveries are deductible too.
  • Compare both methods. Run the numbers using both the simplified and detailed method and claim whichever gives you a higher deduction.
  • Keep your logbook digital. GPS-based apps create audit-proof records that the CRA accepts. Manual logs are more likely to be challenged.
  • Remember the territory bonus. If you drive for business in Northern Canada, you get $0.04/km more than the standard rate.
