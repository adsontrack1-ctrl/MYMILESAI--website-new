Mileage Deduction for Self-Employed: How to Claim Every Mile

The complete 2026 guide for freelancers, 1099 contractors, and sole proprietors who drive for business.

Tax Guide March 12, 2026 9 min read

If you're self-employed and you drive for business, the mileage deduction is one of the largest tax breaks available to you. At the 2026 IRS rate of $0.725 per mile, a consultant who drives 10,000 business miles saves $7,250 on their tax return. A delivery driver logging 20,000 miles? That's $14,500 in deductions.

The key is knowing what qualifies, tracking every mile, and filing correctly on Schedule C.

Who Qualifies as Self-Employed for Mileage

You can claim the mileage deduction if you are any of the following:

  • Sole proprietors — operating a business under your own name or a DBA
  • Independent contractors (1099) — anyone who receives a 1099-NEC instead of a W-2
  • Freelancers — writers, designers, photographers, consultants
  • Gig workers — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, TaskRabbit drivers
  • Single-member LLC owners — taxed as sole proprietors by default
W-2 employees: Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, most W-2 employees cannot deduct unreimbursed business mileage on their federal return. If your employer doesn't reimburse you, ask them to set up a mileage reimbursement program — it's tax-free for both parties when done at or below the IRS rate.

How to Report on Schedule C

Self-employed mileage goes on Schedule C (Form 1040), Line 9 — Car and truck expenses. You'll need to provide:

  • Date the vehicle was placed in service for business
  • Total miles driven during the year
  • Business miles driven
  • Whether the vehicle was available for personal use
  • Whether you have written evidence to support your deduction

The Home Office Advantage

If your home qualifies as your principal place of business (used regularly and exclusively for work), every business trip from your home is deductible from the first mile. Without a home office, only trips that start or end at a business location count.

For many self-employed professionals, establishing a qualifying home office turns your previously non-deductible commute into fully deductible business miles.

Standard Rate vs. Actual Expenses for Self-Employed

As self-employed, you can choose either method each year (with restrictions). The standard rate ($0.725/mile) is simpler — you just multiply miles by the rate. Actual expenses mean tracking every receipt for gas, insurance, maintenance, depreciation, and applying your business-use percentage.

Most self-employed professionals find the standard rate easier and equally beneficial. Run both calculations to see which gives you a bigger deduction.

Self-Employment Tax Impact

Here's what many self-employed people miss: your mileage deduction reduces both your income tax AND your self-employment tax (15.3% for Social Security and Medicare). A $10,000 mileage deduction can save you $1,530 just on SE tax alone, on top of income tax savings.

Record-Keeping Tips for Self-Employed

  • Start tracking on January 1. Don't wait until you "get busy" — the first trip of the year matters.
  • Use an app. GPS-based mileage logs are the gold standard for audit protection.
  • Review weekly. Spend 2 minutes each week classifying any unclassified trips.
  • Record odometer readings. Take a photo of your odometer on January 1 and December 31.
  • Keep the log for 3 years. The IRS can audit returns up to 3 years back (6 years if income is significantly underreported).
