Rideshare Driver Mileage Guide: Uber & Lyft Tax Deductions

How to track and deduct every mile as an Uber or Lyft driver in 2026 — including the miles the apps don't count.

Gig Economy March 20, 2026 9 min read

Here's the thing most rideshare drivers don't realize: Uber and Lyft only report your "online miles" — miles driven with a passenger. But your deductible miles include far more than that. Deadhead miles, en route miles, and miles between rides are all deductible, and they can represent 30-40% of your total driving.

At $0.725/mile, those uncounted miles could be worth $3,000 to $7,000 per year in missed deductions.

The Three Types of Rideshare Miles

1. Trip Miles (Passenger in Car)

Miles driven with a passenger from pickup to drop-off. Uber and Lyft track and report these. Always deductible.

2. En Route Miles (Driving to Pickup)

Miles driven from your current location to the passenger's pickup point after accepting a ride. Deductible but NOT included in Uber's tax summary.

3. Deadhead Miles (Between Rides)

Miles driven after dropping off a passenger while waiting for the next ride request — repositioning to a busy area, driving toward surge zones, or just cruising. These are deductible as long as you're in driver mode and available for rides.

What's NOT Deductible

  • Driving from home to your first pickup area (commute)
  • Driving home after your last ride (commute)
  • Personal trips taken during your shift (food, errands)
  • Miles driven with the app off

Why Uber's Tax Summary Isn't Enough

Uber provides a year-end tax summary with "online miles." But this only counts miles with a passenger. It misses en route miles and deadhead miles. Lyft is similar.

For a driver who logs 30,000 total business miles, the app might only report 18,000 online miles — meaning you'd miss $8,700 in deductions if you only use the app's numbers.

You need a separate GPS mileage tracker that runs alongside Uber/Lyft to capture all business miles.

Other Deductions for Rideshare Drivers

  • Phone and data plan — business percentage of your monthly bill
  • Phone mount, charger, cables — 100% if used only for driving
  • Water and snacks for passengers — business expense
  • Car washes and detailing — business percentage
  • Tolls and parking — deductible on top of the mileage rate
  • Uber/Lyft service fees — deductible as business expenses
  • Safety equipment — dashcam, first aid kit, fire extinguisher

The Home Office Strategy for Rideshare

If you have a space at home where you regularly manage your rideshare business — checking app analytics, managing your schedule, handling taxes — it may qualify as a home office. This makes your drive to the first pickup and drive home from the last drop-off both deductible, potentially adding 3,000-5,000 miles per year to your deductions.

Quarterly Taxes for Rideshare

As a 1099 independent contractor, no taxes are withheld from your rideshare earnings, you need to make quarterly estimated payments to cover income tax and self-employment tax (15.3%). Set aside 25-30% of your net earnings and pay quarterly via Form 1040-ES. Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15.

Canadian Rideshare Drivers

Canadian Uber and Lyft drivers follow CRA rules. You can claim actual vehicle expenses using your business-use percentage, or use the CRA simplified rate of $0.73/km for the first 5,000 km. Keep a CRA-compliant logbook. File on Form T2125. Note: Canadian rideshare drivers must also register for and collect GST/HST.
