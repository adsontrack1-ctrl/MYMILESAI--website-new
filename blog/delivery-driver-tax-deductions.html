Delivery Driver Tax Deductions 2026: The Complete Guide

Every deduction available to DoorDash, Uber Eats, Instacart, Amazon Flex, Grubhub, and Skip The Dishes drivers.

Gig Economy March 15, 2026 11 min read

As a delivery driver, you're an independent contractor — which means you're responsible for your own taxes, but you also have access to powerful deductions that can save you thousands. The biggest one? Mileage. At $0.725 per mile in 2026, a driver logging 25,000 business miles can deduct $18,125.

Here's everything you can deduct, organized from largest to smallest impact.

1. Mileage — Your Biggest Deduction

At $0.725/mile, mileage is almost always your largest tax deduction. Here's what counts:

Deductible miles:

  • Driving to the restaurant or store for pickup
  • Driving from pickup to the customer's address
  • Miles between deliveries (deadhead miles)
  • Driving to a busy zone to wait for orders
  • Driving between platforms if you multi-app

Not deductible:

  • Driving from home to your first delivery zone (this is a commute)
  • Driving home after your last delivery
  • Personal errands during your shift
Pro tip: If you have a qualifying home office (even a desk where you check orders and manage your delivery business), your first trip from home becomes deductible from mile one.

2. Phone and Data Plan

Your smartphone is essential for delivery work. You can deduct the business-use percentage of your phone bill and data plan. If you use your phone 60% for delivery work, deduct 60% of your monthly bill.

3. Delivery Equipment and Supplies

  • Insulated delivery bags and hot bags
  • Phone mounts and car chargers
  • Flashlights for nighttime deliveries
  • Hand sanitizer, gloves, masks
  • Cooler bags for grocery deliveries

4. Parking and Tolls

Parking fees and tolls incurred during deliveries are 100% deductible — and they're deductible on top of the standard mileage rate. Keep receipts or use an app that tracks them.

5. Platform Fees and Commissions

Service fees charged by delivery platforms (DoorDash, Uber, etc.) are deductible business expenses when they're taken from your earnings.

6. Self-Employment Tax Deduction

You pay 15.3% self-employment tax (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on your net earnings. The IRS lets you deduct half of your SE tax on Form 1040, which reduces your adjusted gross income.

7. Health Insurance Premiums

If you're self-employed and pay for your own health insurance, the premiums are deductible on Form 1040. This applies to medical, dental, and vision insurance for you and your family.

Example: How Deductions Add Up

DeductionAmount

Phone (60% of $100/mo × 12)$720
Equipment (bags, mount, charger)$150

On $40,000 gross delivery earnings, these deductions reduce your taxable income to under $23,000 — saving roughly $4,300 in federal taxes.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

As a delivery driver, no taxes are withheld from your pay. You're expected to make quarterly estimated tax payments using Form 1040-ES. Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. Missing these can result in penalties even if you pay in full at filing.

What About Multi-App Drivers?

If you drive for multiple platforms (DoorDash + Uber Eats + Instacart), you track all business miles together — you don't need separate logs per platform. Just ensure every mile is documented. Your total mileage deduction applies against your combined delivery income on a single Schedule C.

Canada: Delivery Driver Deductions

Canadian delivery drivers (Skip The Dishes, Uber Eats, Instacart) follow CRA rules. Instead of the standard mileage rate, you can claim actual vehicle expenses multiplied by your business-use percentage, or use the simplified CRA rate of $0.73/km for the first 5,000 km. Keep a CRA-compliant logbook and file on Form T2125.
