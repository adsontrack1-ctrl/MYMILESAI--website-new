IRS Mileage Rate 2026: Everything You Need to Know

The complete guide to the 2026 standard mileage rate, who qualifies, how to calculate your deduction, and what records the IRS requires.

Tax Rates March 1, 2026 8 min read

The IRS standard mileage rate for 2026 is $0.70 per mile for business use. This is the amount you can deduct per mile driven for business purposes when filing your federal tax return. If you're self-employed, a freelancer, a delivery driver, a real estate agent, or anyone who drives for work, this rate directly affects how much you save at tax time.

The rate is set annually by the IRS based on a study of the fixed and variable costs of operating a vehicle, including gas, insurance, depreciation, maintenance, and repairs. For 2026, the business rate remained at $0.70 per mile, unchanged from 2025.

The charitable rate of $0.14 per mile is set by statute and rarely changes. The medical/moving rate applies only to qualified medical travel expenses or moves by active-duty members of the Armed Forces.

Who Can Claim the Business Mileage Deduction?

The standard mileage rate is available to the following groups:

  • Self-employed individuals — freelancers, independent contractors, sole proprietors, and gig workers (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, etc.) can deduct business miles on Schedule C
  • Small business owners — anyone operating a business and using a personal vehicle for business purposes
  • Real estate agents — miles driven to showings, open houses, client meetings, and property inspections
  • Sales representatives — territory coverage, client visits, and trade shows
  • Medical and pharmaceutical reps — hospital and clinic visits within your territory
  • Delivery drivers — all miles driven while actively delivering or heading to pickup locations
Important: Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, W-2 employees can no longer deduct unreimbursed business mileage on their federal tax return (except active-duty military). If you're an employee, check whether your employer offers a mileage reimbursement program instead.

The calculation is straightforward: multiply your total business miles by the IRS rate.

Standard Mileage Rate vs. Actual Expense Method

The IRS gives you two options for deducting vehicle expenses. You should choose whichever gives you the larger deduction:

Standard Mileage Rate ($0.70/mile): Simpler. You just track your miles and multiply by the rate. This covers gas, oil, repairs, insurance, registration, and depreciation in one flat rate. Best for most people, especially those with newer or fuel-efficient vehicles.

Actual Expense Method: You track all actual vehicle costs (gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, lease payments, etc.) and deduct the business-use percentage. More complex but potentially higher if you drive an expensive vehicle with high maintenance costs. Requires keeping receipts for everything.

For most self-employed professionals, the standard mileage rate is simpler and often results in a comparable or higher deduction. The key is tracking every mile accurately.

What Records Does the IRS Require?

The IRS requires a contemporaneous mileage log — meaning you record trips at or near the time they occur, not from memory at year-end. Your log must include:

  • Date of each trip
  • Destination (where you drove)
  • Business purpose (why you drove — client meeting, delivery, showing, etc.)
  • Miles driven for each trip

You should also record your odometer reading at the start and end of each year to establish total annual mileage.

The IRS can disallow your entire mileage deduction if you don't have adequate records. A mileage tracking app that logs trips automatically with GPS is the most reliable way to maintain a compliant mileage log.

What Miles Are Deductible (and What Aren't)?

Deductible:

  • Driving from your office to a client's location
  • Driving between two work sites
  • Trips to the bank, post office, or supply store for business
  • Driving from a home office (that qualifies) to a client
  • All miles while actively working a gig (delivery, rideshare)
  • Driving to a temporary work location

Not deductible:

  • Your regular commute from home to a fixed workplace
  • Personal errands (grocery store, gym, school pickup)
  • Vacation travel, even if you do some business

Common Mistakes That Cost You Money

Here are the most frequent errors people make with mileage deductions:

  • Not tracking miles at all. The IRS won't accept a guess or estimate. You need a log.
  • Forgetting short trips. A 3-mile drive to the bank doesn't seem like much, but it adds up to hundreds of dollars over a year.
  • Logging commuting miles as business. Your home-to-office commute is never deductible unless you have a qualifying home office.
  • Waiting until December to start tracking. The IRS requires contemporaneous records. Reconstructing a year's worth of trips from memory won't hold up in an audit.
  • Not tracking deadhead miles. For rideshare and delivery drivers, miles driven between deliveries or while heading to pickup locations are deductible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I deduct mileage if I work from home?

If your home office qualifies as your principal place of business (used regularly and exclusively for business), then trips from home to client locations, job sites, or business meetings are deductible from the first mile.

Do I need to track my odometer?

The IRS recommends recording your odometer reading at the beginning and end of each tax year. This helps establish your total annual mileage and the percentage used for business. A GPS tracking app does this automatically.

Can I use the standard mileage rate for more than one vehicle?

Yes. You can use the standard mileage rate for multiple vehicles, but you must track miles separately for each vehicle. You cannot use the standard rate for more than five vehicles used simultaneously (fleet rule).

What if I'm audited?

If audited, the IRS will ask to see your mileage log. Digital logs from a GPS tracking app are generally considered more reliable than handwritten logs because they include timestamps, GPS coordinates, and route data that can be verified.
